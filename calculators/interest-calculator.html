Understanding Simple vs Compound Interest in India

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal amount, while compound interest is calculated on the principal plus any accumulated interest. Over time, compound interest grows your money significantly faster because you earn "interest on interest."

Simple Interest Formula

Simple Interest = P × r × t

Where P is principal, r is annual rate (as decimal), and t is time in years.

Compound Interest Formula

A = P × (1 + r/n)(n×t)

Where A is the maturity amount, P is principal, r is annual rate, n is compounding frequency per year, and t is time in years. Indian banks typically compound quarterly for savings accounts and Fixed Deposits.

Why Compounding Frequency Matters

Higher compounding frequency (daily > monthly > quarterly > annually) gives slightly higher returns. For example, ₹5 lakh at 8% for 5 years gives ₹7.00 lakh with annual compounding vs ₹7.43 lakh with quarterly compounding. Frequently Asked Questions – Interest Calculator

What is the difference between simple and compound interest?
Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. Compound interest is calculated on principal + accumulated interest. Over 10 years, compound interest will give you significantly more returns than simple interest on the same amount.
Which compounding frequency gives the highest returns?
Daily compounding gives the highest returns, followed by monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, and annually. However, the difference between daily and monthly is usually very small (a few hundred rupees on ₹5 lakh over 5 years).
How do Indian banks calculate interest?
Most Indian banks use quarterly compounding for savings accounts and Fixed Deposits. Home loans use the reducing balance method (monthly compounding). RBI mandates quarterly compounding on savings accounts since April 2010.
What is the Rule of 72?
The Rule of 72 is a quick way to estimate how long your money takes to double. Divide 72 by the interest rate. At 8%, your money doubles in approximately 72/8 = 9 years. At 12%, it doubles in about 6 years.
Is compound interest always better than simple interest?
For investments and deposits, yes — compound interest grows your money faster. For loans, simple interest is better for the borrower. Most bank loans in India use compound interest (reducing balance), which is why our EMI Calculator is essential for planning loan repayments.
